Learn English Tenses Easily

Tenses in English grammar express the time of action or event. There are 16 major English tenses, divided into four main categories: Simple, Continuous (Progressive), Perfect, and Perfect Continuous. Each tense describes different aspects of time and duration of actions.

The 16 tenses are:

  • Simple Present â€“ Describes habits, general facts, or regular actions.
    Example: She drinks coffee every morning.

  • Present Continuous â€“ Describes actions happening right now or near future plans.
    Example: He is reading a book now.

  • Present Perfect â€“ Describes actions that happened in the past but are still relevant or have effects now.
    Example: I have finished my homework.

  • Present Perfect Continuous â€“ Describes actions that started in the past and are still continuing.
    Example: She has been studying for three hours.

  • Simple Past â€“ Describes completed actions in the past.
    Example: They visited Bali last year.

  • Past Continuous â€“ Describes actions that were in progress at a certain time in the past.
    Example: I was cooking when he called.

  • Past Perfect â€“ Describes actions that were completed before another past action.
    Example: She had left when I arrived.

  • Past Perfect Continuous â€“ Describes continuous actions that were happening before another event in the past.
    Example: He had been working for two hours before lunch.

  • Simple Future â€“ Describes actions that will happen in the future.
    Example: I will travel to Japan next month.

  • Future Continuous â€“ Describes actions that will be in progress at a certain time in the future.
    Example: She will be studying at 8 PM.

  • Future Perfect â€“ Describes actions that will be completed before a specific time in the future.
    Example: We will have finished the project by tomorrow.

  • Future Perfect Continuous â€“ Describes actions that will have been happening for a period of time by a certain future point.
    Example: By next year, they will have been living here for a decade.

  • Past Future Simple â€“ Describes a past intention or prediction about the future.
    Example: He said he would call me.

  • Past Future Continuous â€“ Describes actions that were expected to be in progress at a certain time in the future, from a past point of view.
    Example: She said she would be working at that time.

    Example: I thought I would have finished the report by then.

  • Past Future Perfect Continuous â€“ Describes ongoing actions that were expected to have continued for a period of time by a future point, from a past point of view.

English Tenses Explained with Formulae

Tense Description Formula Example (Positive) Example (Negative) Example (Interrogative)
Simple Present Used for habitual actions, general truths or repeated events. Positive:
Subject + base verb (s/es for he/she/it)

Negative:
Subject + do/does + not + base verb

Interrogative:
Do/Does + subject + base verb ? 		She walks to school every day. She does not walk to school on Sundays. Does she walk to school?
Present Continuous Used for actions happening now or around the present time. Positive:
Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + am/is/are + not + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Am/Is/Are + subject + verb-ing ? 		He is reading a book right now. He is not reading a newspaper. Is he reading the book?
Present Perfect Used for actions that happened at an unspecified time before now or started in the past and continue to the present. Positive:
Subject + have/has + past participle

Negative:
Subject + have/has + not + past participle

Interrogative:
Have/Has + subject + past participle ? 		They have visited Paris several times. They have not visited London yet. Have they visited Paris?
Present Perfect Continuous Used to show that an action started in the past and has continued up until now. Positive:
Subject + have/has + been + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + have/has + not + been + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Have/Has + subject + been + verb-ing ? 		She has been working here for five years. She has not been working today. Has she been working long?
Simple Past Used for completed actions at a specific time in the past. Positive:
Subject + past form of verb

Negative:
Subject + did + not + base verb

Interrogative:
Did + subject + base verb ? 		I watched a movie yesterday. I did not watch TV last night. Did you watch that movie?
Past Continuous Used for actions that were happening at a specific time in the past. Positive:
Subject + was/were + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + was/were + not + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Was/Were + subject + verb-ing ? 		They were playing football at 5 PM. They were not playing basketball then. Were they playing football?
Past Perfect Used to show that an action was completed before another took place in the past. Positive:
Subject + had + past participle

Negative:
Subject + had + not + past participle

Interrogative:
Had + subject + past participle ? 		She had left before I arrived. She had not left when I called. Had she left before you came?
Past Perfect Continuous Used to show duration of an action up to a certain point in the past. Positive:
Subject + had + been + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + had + not + been + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Had + subject + been + verb-ing ? 		He had been studying for two hours when she called. He had not been studying before dinner. Had he been studying long before she arrived?
Simple Future Used for actions that will happen in the future. Positive:
Subject + will + base verb

Negative:
Subject + will + not + base verb

Interrogative:
Will + subject + base verb ? 		I will travel to Japan next month. I will not travel next week. Will you travel tomorrow?
Future Continuous Used for actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future. Positive:
Subject + will + be + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + will + not + be + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Will + subject + be + verb-ing ? 		She will be working at 10 AM tomorrow. She will not be working on Sunday. Will she be working tomorrow morning?
Future Perfect Used to describe an action that will be completed before a certain time in the future. Positive:
Subject + will + have + past participle

Negative:
Subject + will + not + have + past participle

Interrogative:
Will + subject + have + past participle ? 		They will have finished the project by Friday. They will not have finished by tomorrow. Will they have finished on time?
Future Perfect Continuous Used to show that an action will have been happening for a duration by a future time. Positive:
Subject + will + have + been + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + will + not + have + been + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Will + subject + have + been + verb-ing ? 		By next year, I will have been living here for a decade. By next year, I will not have been living here for very long. Will you have been living here for 10 years by then?
Past Future Simple Used to describe a future action from a past perspective. Positive:
Subject + would + base verb

Negative:
Subject + would + not + base verb

Interrogative:
Would + subject + base verb ? 		She said she would travel tomorrow. She said she would not travel on Monday. Would she travel the next day?
Past Future Continuous Used to describe an ongoing action in the future from a past viewpoint. Positive:
Subject + would + be + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + would + not + be + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Would + subject + be + verb-ing ? 		He said he would be working at 5 PM. He said he would not be working then. Would he be working at that time?
Past Future Perfect Used to describe a completed action in the future from a past perspective. Positive:
Subject + would + have + past participle

Negative:
Subject + would + not + have + past participle

Interrogative:
Would + subject + have + past participle ? 		They said they would have left by noon. They said they would not have left by 1 PM. Would they have left by noon?
Past Future Perfect Continuous Used to describe a continuing action in the future up to a certain point from a past perspective. Positive:
Subject + would + have + been + verb-ing

Negative:
Subject + would + not + have + been + verb-ing

Interrogative:
Would + subject + have + been + verb-ing ? 		By then, she would have been working for hours. By then, she would not have been working for so long. Would she have been working for hours by then?
